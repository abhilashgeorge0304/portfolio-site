### A Presentation About Presentations

Over the years — pitching at the Young Innovators Programme, defending EcoSift at Dekathon, competing in Present Around the World — I kept noticing the same thing. The teams that won weren't the ones with the best ideas. They were the ones where the room went quiet. Where judges stopped writing and started listening.

The teams that lost had data, solutions, perfectly formatted decks. They opened with "Good afternoon, today we will present our project on..." and the room stayed polite and distant until the timer ran out.

I wanted to understand the gap. Not the content gap — the *attention* gap. And the answer didn't come from a textbook on public speaking. It came from watching films differently.

Every film you've ever watched follows the same invisible arc. I call it the **4C framework**: **Context, Conflict, Climax, Closure** — with rising action building tension and falling action bringing it home. You already know this structure. You just don't see it because good storytelling hides itself.

I'm going to make it visible. And I'm going to use it right now, in this article, while teaching it to you. Pay attention to what you *feel* as you read — not just what you learn.

Let me tell you about a mother.

---

### Context: Make Them Care Before You Break Anything

The first C. Before any conflict, any drama, any reason to care — the audience needs to understand what "normal" looks like. What's the world? Who lives in it? What's at stake if things go wrong?

In Avengers: Endgame, the first act isn't a battle. It's grief. The Avengers are broken. Half the universe is gone. You spend twenty minutes watching them fail at moving on. That's context — it establishes what was lost so you care about getting it back.

In a Bollywood love story, the first fifteen minutes are the campus, the friends, the bickering that's clearly going somewhere. Normal life, before everything goes sideways.

In a presentation, your context slides do the same work. What's the background? Why should anyone in this room care? Don't rush past this. If the audience doesn't feel the world you're describing, the problem you introduce next is just an abstraction they'll forget by lunch.

### Conflict: Introduce the Dragon

The second C. Something changes. A problem appears that can't be ignored. This is where the story *begins* — everything before it was prologue.

A conflict isn't a fact. "The education sector faces challenges" is a fact nobody cares about. A conflict has tension. Two forces pulling in opposite directions. A question the audience wants answered.

In Endgame, the conflict isn't "Thanos is bad." It's "we have exactly one shot at undoing this, and it requires risking everything we have left." That's tension. The question hangs: will they pull it off?

In the Bollywood love story, the conflict isn't "families disapprove." It's the pull between love and loyalty — two things the characters value equally, forced into opposition. The audience doesn't just understand the problem. They feel it in their chest.

Your conflict slide should do the same. Don't state the problem. Make the audience *feel* it.

### Rising Action: Don't Give Them the Answer Yet

This is the space between the problem and the solution. Amateurs skip it. Professionals live in it.

Don't jump from "here's the problem" to "here's our solution." The audience needs to feel the *weight* of the problem before the solution can feel like relief. Show what's been tried. Show why it didn't work. Show why the obvious fixes fall short. Each slide should make the audience more desperate for an answer.

In Endgame, the rising action is the time heist — the planning, the travel across timelines, the near-misses, the sacrifice. Each setback raises the stakes. By the time the final battle begins, the audience isn't watching. They're *invested.* They need it to work.

In Bollywood, the rising action is the longest act. The secret meetings. The dancing (always the dancing). The families escalating. The audience knows love will win — but the rising action makes them doubt it just enough.

### Climax: The Moment Everything Was Building Toward

The third C. The peak. Your idea, your strategy, your answer to the question the audience has been holding.

If the rising action did its job, the climax doesn't need to be loud. It just needs to arrive. "I am Iron Man" — three words carrying the weight of twenty-three films. The climax works because of everything that came before it. Skip the buildup and jump straight to your solution slide — nobody cares. Build the tension first, and the same slide lands like a revelation.

In the Bollywood love story, the climax often hits earlier than you'd expect. Love triumphs at the interval. The falling action takes up the second half — celebration, one last obstacle, the final dance. Different shape from Hollywood. Same emotional payoff.

The less you put on a climax slide, the harder it hits. Let the silence do the work. The audience has already built the answer in their heads — you just need to confirm it.

### Closure: Land It

The fourth C. Don't trail off. Don't end on "any questions?" Land the plane.

The closure shows the audience the story is complete — and real. Execution plan. Next steps. What you need from them. A closed loop with a door left open.

### How to Build It: Four Phases

The 4C framework is the skeleton. Here's how you put flesh on it — borrowed from how movies are actually made.

**Development.** Before you open PowerPoint, figure out the story. What's the one takeaway? Where's the conflict? Where's the climax? Sketch the arc on paper. A director doesn't start filming without a screenplay. You shouldn't start designing slides without a narrative.

**Production.** Create slides, one per story beat. Every slide earns its place or gets cut. If it doesn't advance the narrative, it goes — no matter how good the data looks on it.

**Post-production.** Edit ruthlessly. Clear titles on every slide. Strip clutter. Visual aids that complement the story, not compete with it. A good film editor cuts 30% of the footage. Be willing to kill your darlings.

**Delivery.** Open body language. Eye contact across the room, not just the front row. Pace your speech — slow down for key points, speed up for energy. The pause before a climax slide is the film score going quiet before the explosion. You're not reading slides to an audience. You're performing a story for them.
