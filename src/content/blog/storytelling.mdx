### Most Presentations Are Forgettable. Here's Why.

Think about your day-to-day life. What do you actually remember? Not the facts. Not the data. Not the textbook definitions you memorised for an exam. What sticks are stories. The time something went wrong and someone fixed it. The moment that surprised you. The conversation that changed how you thought about something.

Stories are ubiquitous — shared across every culture on the planet. They're timeless — cavemen painted hunting stories on cave walls. They're contagious — great stories get retold billions of times. They're memorable — you forget the lecture, but you remember the anecdote. And most importantly, they inspire. Martin Luther King didn't change America with a PowerPoint deck. He told a story about a dream.

Over years of presenting at hackathons, competitions, and college events — pitching Athena to investors at Keraleeyam, presenting EcoSift at Dekathon, competing in Present Around the World — I noticed that the presentations that won weren't necessarily the ones with the best ideas. They were the ones that told the best stories. The ones where the audience leaned forward, not because the slide was pretty, but because they wanted to know what happened next.

That observation sent me looking for a structure. And I found it in an unexpected place.

### The 4C Framework: Stolen from the Movies

Every movie you've ever watched follows the same arc. Every single one. Context, Conflict, Climax, Closure — with rising action building tension toward the peak, and falling action resolving it afterward.

**Context** — the setup. Who are we, where are we, what's normal?

**Conflict** — the disruption. Something changes. A problem appears. An opportunity emerges. This is where the story begins.

**Rising action** — tension builds. Stakes increase. The audience gets invested because they want to see how it resolves.

**Climax** — the peak. The moment everything has been building toward. The battle. The decision. The revelation.

**Falling action → Closure** — the resolution. Loose ends tied up. The audience exhales.

If you don't believe this applies to everything, take Avengers: Endgame. Context: there's a villain who wants to destroy half the universe. Conflict: the heroes have one chance to stop him. Rising action: time travel, skirmishes, sacrifice. Climax: the final battle. Closure: the aftermath (and yes, someone dies). Every beat follows the framework.

Bollywood does the same thing, just with more dancing. Boy meets girl. They hate each other. They fall in love. Families disapprove. Fighting, dramatic songs, rising tension. Love triumphs — climax. Happy ending — closure.

The structure isn't a creative choice. It's how human brains process narrative. We need setup to understand context. We need conflict to care. We need a climax to feel satisfied. And we need closure to walk away feeling complete.

### Applying the 4C Framework to Presentations

The same structure that makes movies work makes presentations work. Here's how it maps:

**Context slides** — set the stage. What's the background? Why are we here? What's the current state of things? Don't rush this. If the audience doesn't understand the world you're describing, they won't care about the problem you're solving.

**Conflict slides** — introduce the problem, the tension, the opportunity. This is where most bad presentations start and end — "here's a problem." Good presentations make the audience *feel* the problem. Use data. Use a story. Use a question that doesn't have an obvious answer.

**Rising action** — the slides between the problem and the solution. Don't jump straight to your answer. Build toward it. Show what you explored. Show what didn't work. Show why the obvious solutions fall short. Every slide should increase the audience's anticipation for what's coming next.

**Climax** — the aha moment. This is your idea, your strategy, your solution, your finding. Everything before this was building toward it. If you've done the rising action well, the audience is ready for it — and it lands with impact.

**Falling action → Closure** — execution plan, next steps, future scope. The audience has seen the peak. Now show them how it comes to life. End with something concrete — a timeline, a number, a commitment. Don't trail off.

### Making the Story: A Four-Phase Process

Knowing the framework is one thing. Actually building a story-driven presentation is another. I think of it in four phases — borrowed, again, from how movies are made.

**Development.** This is your rough cut. Brainstorm the key message — what's the one thing you want people to take away? Decide on your story arc. Sketch the flow. Write a basic script. Don't open your slide software yet. The story comes first, the slides come second.

**Production.** Now create your slides, aligning each one with a stage of the 4C framework. Every slide should serve the narrative. If a slide doesn't move the story forward, cut it. If you're using analogies or examples, make sure they hook into the main arc rather than distracting from it.

**Post-production.** Edit and refine. Give every slide a clear, non-ambiguous title that tells the audience what they're looking at. Strip out clutter — busy slides with too many colours, too much text, and too many bullet points kill narrative momentum. Use visual aids that complement the story, not compete with it.

**Delivery.** This is where the story lives or dies. Body language matters — open arms signal confidence, crossed arms signal defensiveness. Make eye contact with different parts of the room, not just the front row. Pace your speech: slowing down emphasises key points, speeding up injects energy and holds attention. You're not reading slides to an audience. You're performing a story for them.

The article practiced what it preached. You didn't notice because that's what good storytelling does. It doesn't announce itself. It just makes you want to keep reading until the end.

Every presentation you give from now on is a story. The framework is simple: context, conflict, climax, closure. Build the narrative before you build the slides. And when you deliver, remember — people don't want to be told things. They want to be inspired.

The best compliment I ever received after a competition presentation wasn't "great data" or "nice slides." It was someone saying, "I wanted to know what happened next."
