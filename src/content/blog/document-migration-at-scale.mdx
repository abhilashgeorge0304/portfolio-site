### The Problem No One Plans For

When an organization decides to wind down a line of business and transfer its portfolio to another servicer, the data migration playbook is well understood: extract account records, transform them to the receiving platform's format, validate, and ship. Teams plan for it. Vendors have tools for it. It's hard, but it's tractable.

What nobody plans for is the documents.

Not the structured data — the actual *documents*. The promissory notes, the cosigner addendums, the final disclosures, the tax forms, the statements, the correspondence. The physical and digital artifacts that prove a borrower agreed to specific terms, that a cosigner accepted liability, that a disclosure was delivered on a particular date. In a consumer lending business, these documents aren't nice-to-haves. They're legally required evidence of the lending relationship — and when completeness expectations accompany a portfolio sale, "we think we have most of them somewhere" is not an adequate position.

We faced exactly this situation. Our organization was winding down its student lending operation and transferring the full portfolio — roughly two million borrower accounts — to a new servicer. The account data transfer was complex but conventional. The document transfer was something else entirely: approximately 200 million documents, scattered across five different source systems, accumulated over more than 25 years, with metadata that ranged from pristine to nonexistent. The transfer required near-complete document delivery — not as a best-effort target, but as an expectation tied to the portfolio sale. Priority documents — promissory notes, disclosures, addendums — had to arrive on the *day* of each transfer tranche. Everything else within 45 days.

This article walks through the architecture we built to solve that problem: how we centralized fragmented metadata, built a three-tier matching strategy that deliberately exhausted cheap methods before deploying expensive ones, and used a RAG pipeline — embedding-based semantic search augmented by a small language model — to resolve the residual that structured approaches couldn't reach, with human-in-the-loop validation ensuring that every AI-assisted match decision had governance behind it.

### Why Document Migration Is Harder Than Data Migration

Before diving into the solution, it's worth understanding why this problem is structurally different from — and arguably harder than — migrating structured account data.

**Multiple storage paradigms across decades.** Our documents lived in five fundamentally different systems. The loan origination system stored application-stage documents in a relational database, indexed by application ID. The servicing platform generated statements automatically and stored them in an enterprise archive with a proprietary format. A content management system held inbound customer mail and agent-uploaded documents with free-form categorization. An external print vendor retained outbound disclosures as daily bulk PDF files with minimal indexing. And an enterprise document vault stored servicing-triggered letters in a date-batched archive format that required API-based retrieval. Each system had its own identifier scheme, its own categorization taxonomy, its own storage format, and its own gaps.

**Metadata inconsistency compounds over time.** Over 25 years, the way documents were tagged, categorized, and associated with accounts changed multiple times. Early-vintage loans had documents with borrower ID only — no loan-level association. Mid-vintage loans introduced application IDs but with inconsistent formats. Some systems used free-text document categories; others used structured codes. A document tagged as "Application - Promissory Note" in one system might appear as "PNote" in another, or as "Other Servicing Document" in a third because an agent selected the wrong category during manual upload. These weren't edge cases — they represented a meaningful portion of the total population.

**Completeness tolerance is essentially zero for priority documents.** Missing a few fields in a data migration file produces a validation error that gets caught in testing. Missing a promissory note means a legally required document isn't where it needs to be. We needed a framework that could systematically identify *every* gap and resolve as many as possible before transfer day — and we needed an audit trail that could demonstrate, if ever asked, exactly what we found, how we matched it, and what the confidence level was for each decision.

### The Four-Phase Migration Framework

We structured the migration as a four-phase execution framework, where each phase had a clear deliverable and a defined "done" criteria. The phases were designed to run with some overlap — metadata loading could begin while gap analysis tooling was still being built — but each phase's output was a prerequisite for the next.

<img src="/images/blog/document-migration/four-phase-approach.svg" alt="Four-phase migration framework: Load, Analyze Gaps, Centralize, and Transfer" />
<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 3: The four-phase execution framework — each phase has a specific deliverable, and priority documents must be ready by Day 1 of each transfer tranche.
</span>

**Phase 1 — Load.** Extract metadata from each source system and ingest it into the enterprise data warehouse. Each source team produced a metadata file in a standardized format: customer identifier, document type, creation date, document source, and a pointer to the physical document's storage location. The key challenge here was normalization — dates came in different formats, document type taxonomies didn't align, and some systems used surrogate keys that had to be mapped back to the master account identifier. We invested significant effort in building a translation layer that harmonized these differences before loading into a central staging area.

**Phase 2 — Analyze Gaps.** With metadata loaded, we could run our matching algorithms and immediately see what was missing. For each account, we compared the matched document inventory against a mandatory checklist — the set of document types that *should* exist based on the loan's characteristics (origination date, cosigner status, program type). The gap analysis produced a matrix: account by document type, with each cell flagged as matched, missing, or not applicable. This matrix became the operational heartbeat of the program — updated weekly, reviewed by workstream leads, and tracked against completeness targets.

**Phase 3 — Centralize.** Matched and gap-resolved documents were migrated from their source systems into a centralized staging repository. Physical format conversion happened here (proprietary archive format to PDF, flat files to structured output). Transfer rosters were generated in the industry-standard format required by the receiving servicer. An immutable metadata table was created in the data warehouse as a permanent audit trail — recording every document's source, match method, confidence score, and transfer status — so that if the completeness of the transfer was ever questioned by auditors or regulators, the full lineage was available for any document at any point in the future.

**Phase 4 — Transfer.** Execute the actual file transfer, aligned with each account conversion tranche. The receiving servicer validated file structure and document counts. Post-transfer reconciliation ensured that what we sent matched what they received. Any discrepancies triggered immediate investigation.

### Building the Central Metadata Repository

The central metadata repository was the single most important architectural decision in the entire program. Without it, gap analysis would have required querying five separate systems in real-time — a logistical impossibility given the volumes involved and the security constraints around some of the source systems.

The repository lived in the enterprise data warehouse as a set of structured tables. The core table held one row per document-account association, with fields for: customer identifier (CIF), loan identifier, application identifier, document type (normalized), document source system, document creation date, physical storage location (a pointer, not the document itself), and a match confidence indicator.

Populating this repository required coordination across five source teams, each of which had to extract their metadata, map it to the standard format, and push it through a secure file transfer pipeline into the warehouse. The data warehouse team — our team — then ran the standardization, deduplication, and matching processes.

The structure of the metadata that would eventually be shared with the receiving servicer followed a defined specification: borrower identifiers, name fields, document type code, loan identifier, date received, sale/transfer date, a deal identifier, and the physical file name. Every one of these fields was required, and the specification was strict about formats and lengths. Building our central repository with this output format in mind meant we could generate transfer-ready files directly from the warehouse without an additional transformation step.

### The Cost-Management Insight: Three Tiers of Resolution

The core technical problem was this: given a document with some metadata, determine which of the two million accounts it belongs to. When the metadata was rich and consistent, this was straightforward. When it wasn't, we needed progressively more sophisticated — and more expensive — approaches.

We designed a three-tier resolution strategy, and the design was as much about *cost and complexity management* as it was about accuracy. Deterministic matching is essentially free (SQL joins). Probabilistic matching is computationally cheap (scoring functions). The RAG pipeline is expensive per document (embedding generation, vector search, SLM inference). By reserving the most expensive approach for the residual that cheaper methods couldn't resolve, we kept the AI footprint small enough to be practical — both in compute cost and in the human review effort required to validate results. In our case, this cascade reduced the GenAI scope by roughly 30×.

<img src="/images/blog/document-migration/matching-strategy.svg" alt="Three-tier matching strategy showing deterministic, probabilistic, and RAG pipeline resolution with cumulative match rates" />
<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 4: The three-tier matching strategy — deterministic exact-key joins resolve the majority, probabilistic scoring handles the next layer, and the RAG pipeline tackles the long tail. Each tier reduces the volume that flows to the next.
</span>

**Tier 1: Deterministic Matching.** The first pass used exact-key joins. If a document's metadata contained a customer identifier (CIF) or application ID that mapped directly to an account in the master table, the match was automatic. This resolved roughly 85% of the total population. The high match rate was driven primarily by the servicing platform and document vault, which had well-structured metadata with reliable identifiers. The origination system and content management system had significantly lower deterministic match rates, particularly for older vintages.

**Tier 2: Probabilistic Matching.** For the ~15% that didn't match deterministically, we built a probabilistic scoring engine. This used a weighted combination of partial identifiers: last four digits of SSN, borrower name similarity (Jaro-Winkler distance), document creation date proximity to known account events (origination date, first payment date), and document type heuristics (e.g., a cosigner addendum should only match to accounts that had a cosigner). Each signal contributed to a composite confidence score between 0 and 100. Matches scoring above 80 were accepted automatically; matches between 50 and 80 were flagged for review. This tier resolved approximately 12% of the remaining population, bringing the cumulative match rate to approximately 97%.

**Tier 3: The RAG Pipeline.** The remaining ~3% was the hard residual — documents with severely incomplete metadata, mismatched identifiers, or content from legacy systems where the tagging was unreliable. Critically, many of these documents were scanned images or legacy PDFs where *the only identifying information existed in the document text itself*, not in the structured metadata. A promissory note from 2003 might have no CIF, no application ID, and an incorrect document category — but the text of the document contained a borrower name and a date that could be matched to an account if you could *read* it. This is the class of problem where traditional record linkage approaches, no matter how sophisticated, reach their ceiling — and where semantic understanding of document content becomes genuinely necessary.

### The Tier 3 RAG Pipeline: Retrieve, Augment, Generate

The insight behind Tier 3 is this: when structured metadata is insufficient to identify a match, the *content* of the document — OCR-extracted text from scanned PDFs, fragments of names and dates embedded in the body — may contain enough signal to find the right account. But only if you can search across millions of accounts using semantic similarity rather than exact-key lookups, and only if you can explain *why* a match was made — not just report a similarity score.

Every Tier 3 document runs through the same pipeline, and that pipeline follows the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pattern end to end: top candidate accounts are *retrieved* from the vector database via semantic search, those candidates are passed as context that *augments* the prompt to a small language model (SLM), and the SLM *generates* a match decision, a confidence score, and a natural-language explanation. The confidence score then determines the routing — auto-accept or human review queue — but the explanation is generated for *every* document, regardless of confidence, because it serves the audit trail.

<img src="/images/blog/document-migration/rag-architecture.svg" alt="Tier 3 RAG pipeline showing indexing, retrieval, augmentation, and generation steps, with confidence-based routing to auto-accept or human review queue — both paths writing to an immutable audit trail" />
<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 5: The Tier 3 RAG pipeline — every unmatched document runs through the full retrieve-augment-generate cycle. The SLM generates a match decision and explanation for all documents; high-confidence matches are auto-accepted, everything else goes to a human review queue prioritized by confidence score. Both paths write to an immutable audit trail.
</span>

**Why text assembly is the real engineering.** The embedding model doesn't compare database fields — it compares *text*. The quality of the similarity search depends almost entirely on how you construct the text representations that get embedded. For each account, we assembled a labeled text block: `Borrower: James A. Smith / SSN Suffix: 4523 / Origination: 2015-03-10 / Program: Undergraduate / Missing: Promissory Note`. For each unmatched document, we assembled whatever metadata was available using the same labeling conventions, plus OCR-extracted text from the physical document: `Doc Type: Promissory Note / Created: 2015-03-12 / OCR: "James Smith hereby promises to repay... undergraduate program... disbursement date March 10 2015"`. The field labels serve as semantic anchors — they tell the model what role each piece of information plays, enabling it to recognize that "Borrower: James Smith" in the account text and "James Smith hereby promises to repay" in the OCR text are references to the same person. This template consistency is what makes cross-format similarity search work: a structured account profile and an unstructured scanned document land in nearby regions of the vector space because the underlying semantic content overlaps, even though the formats are completely different.

**The Indexing Pipeline (Batch).** Each account's labeled text block was run through an embedding model to produce a 1536-dimensional vector. These vectors were indexed in our enterprise vector database using an HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) index optimized for approximate nearest-neighbor search. We already had a vector database instance in the environment from other use cases, which significantly reduced setup overhead — we simply provisioned a new index for this project.

**The Query Pipeline (Per Document).** For each unmatched document, we encoded its assembled text as a query vector using the same embedding model and retrieved the top-K most semantically similar accounts by cosine similarity.

**The SLM Step: Disambiguation and Explanation.** The retrieved candidate accounts — along with their similarity scores, the document metadata, and the OCR text — were passed into a small language model (7B parameter class, running batch inference on a single GPU instance). The SLM evaluated the candidates and produced three outputs: a match decision (which candidate, if any), a confidence score, and a natural-language explanation. For high-confidence cases where one candidate clearly dominated, the SLM confirmed the match and articulated why: "Matched to Account #A-8821. Borrower name 'James Smith' matches OCR-extracted text. Origination date 2015-03-10 is within 2 days of document creation. SSN suffix matches: ****4523." For ambiguous cases where multiple candidates scored similarly, the SLM performed fine-grained attribute comparison — "candidate #2's origination date falls within the document's date range while candidate #1's is 8 months away" — and selected the strongest match with its reasoning. The explanation was generated for every document, regardless of confidence level, because it serves the audit trail that any examiner would later review.

**Confidence-Based Routing.** The SLM's confidence score determined what happened next. High-confidence matches were auto-accepted, with the explanation logged directly to the audit trail — no human review required unless spot-checked. Everything else was routed to a single human review queue, prioritized by confidence score, where analysts saw the document, the candidate profiles, and the SLM's explanation, and made the final determination. Both paths wrote to the same immutable audit log.

### Human-in-the-Loop: Not a Fallback, a Feature

The human-in-the-loop (HITL) component was not a concession to imperfect automation. It was a deliberate architectural decision that served two purposes: better accuracy and governance.

**Better accuracy.** For documents in the review queue, analysts saw the document metadata, the top candidate account profiles, and the SLM-generated explanation articulating why the model chose a particular match. They could approve, reject, or reassign. The queue was prioritized by confidence score — borderline cases surfaced first, giving reviewers the ability to triage efficiently. This collaboration — vector retrieval for candidate identification, SLM for reasoning and explanation, human for final judgment — produced outcomes that were better than any single component operating alone. Analysts reported that the SLM explanation reduced review time from approximately five minutes per document (raw metadata, no context) to roughly 45 seconds (pre-analyzed, with reasoning). The system made the human more effective, not redundant.

**Governance and auditability.** Every decision — auto-accepted or human-adjudicated — was logged to an immutable audit trail: document identifier, matched account, match method (deterministic / probabilistic / RAG-auto / RAG-reviewed / manual), confidence score, SLM explanation text, reviewer ID (for human decisions), and timestamp. This wasn't built for a specific audit or examination. It was built to ensure that if the completeness or accuracy of the document transfer was ever questioned — whether six months or six years later — the full decision lineage was there for every document, ready to answer whatever question was asked. Crucially, the SLM-generated explanation meant that even auto-accepted matches had a human-readable rationale on file — not just a similarity score.

The HITL design reflected a principle we think applies broadly to AI-assisted decision-making in regulated environments: the goal isn't to remove humans from the loop — it's to make the loop faster, more informed, and fully traceable.

### Deploying for a One-Off Migration

A portfolio migration is inherently a time-boxed, one-off event. We are not matching documents to accounts on an ongoing basis — we are doing it once, thoroughly, and then retiring the tooling. This shaped every deployment decision.

<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 6: Deployment reality — the one-off migration optimized for time-to-resolution with lightweight tooling, while a recurring workflow would require production-grade orchestration, monitoring, and drift detection.
</span>

**What we actually built.** The enterprise data warehouse served as the data backbone. Batch Python scripts handled embedding generation and similarity scoring, running as scheduled jobs. The vector database was an existing enterprise instance — we provisioned a new index rather than deploying new infrastructure. The SLM (7B parameter class, quantized) ran batch inference on a single GPU instance within our private cloud — no multi-GPU cluster, no load balancer, no API gateway. The total Tier 3 volume was roughly 250K documents; at ~700 tokens per round trip, the full batch completed in a single overnight run. The human review application was purpose-built for this migration — a lightweight web interface with queues, approve/reject workflows, and direct writes to the audit log. No container orchestration. No model monitoring dashboards. No drift detection.

**The key principle: optimize for time-to-resolution, not operational robustness.** We needed the system to process the unmatched residual as accurately as possible within the migration timeline, not to run indefinitely. Every engineering decision was made through that lens — simpler tooling, fewer moving parts, faster iteration when something needed adjustment.

**What a recurring workflow would look like.** If this were a recurring process — for example, an ongoing document classification system that continuously matches incoming documents to accounts — the deployment architecture would look quite different. You'd want a containerized embedding service with GPU backing for consistent throughput. A managed vector database with persistence, high availability, and automated backups. A pipeline orchestrator (Airflow, Step Functions) with retry logic, alerting, and DAG management. Model monitoring to detect embedding drift and confidence score degradation over time. And a continuous feedback loop where human review decisions retrain or fine-tune the scoring model. The infrastructure overhead is substantially higher, but it's justified when the system needs to run reliably for months or years. For a one-off migration, it would have been overengineering.

### Completeness Reporting and Audit Infrastructure

Matching documents to accounts was only half the problem. The other half was knowing *what should be there* and being able to prove, at any point in the future, that the transfer was complete and every decision was traceable.

<img src="/images/blog/document-migration/gap-analysis.svg" alt="Mandatory document checklist, completeness matrix by account and document type, and gap resolution waterfall showing progressive reduction" />
<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 7: Completeness framework — mandatory documents are checked against each account, coverage is tracked in a matrix by document type, and a resolution waterfall progressively closes gaps through multiple strategies.
</span>

**Mandatory document coverage.** The mandatory document checklist varied by loan characteristics. Every borrower account needed a promissory note and a final disclosure. Accounts with cosigners additionally required a cosigner addendum and cosigner disclosure. Certain program types required custom graduation addendums. Tax forms were required for all accounts. Statements had to be present for recent periods. The completeness matrix — account by document type, updated weekly — became the operational heartbeat of the program. It surfaced coverage rates by document type, by vintage year, and by source system, making it immediately visible where the remaining gaps concentrated.

**Match-rate reporting by tier and method.** Every matched document carried a tag indicating *how* it was matched: deterministic (Tier 1), probabilistic (Tier 2), RAG-auto (Tier 3, auto-accepted), or RAG-reviewed (Tier 3, human-approved). This breakdown served two purposes. Operationally, it showed us where each tier was contributing — and where a tier was underperforming relative to expectation (for example, when deterministic match rates for a particular source system were lower than projected, it flagged a metadata quality issue that needed investigation upstream). For governance, it gave a clear picture of how much of the final matched population relied on AI-assisted methods versus traditional joins, and what proportion had been reviewed by a human.

**The immutable audit trail.** Every match decision — across all three tiers — was logged to an immutable table in the data warehouse: document identifier, matched account, match method, confidence score, SLM explanation text (for Tier 3), reviewer ID (for human-adjudicated matches), and timestamp. This wasn't built for a specific examination or regulatory request. It was built so that if *any* question was asked about *any* document — six months or six years after the transfer — the full decision lineage was immediately available. The explanation field was particularly valuable: for Tier 3 matches, it provided a human-readable rationale that a non-technical examiner could evaluate, not just a numerical score.

**Gap resolution tracking.** Where mandatory documents were missing after all three matching tiers, the resolution waterfall tracked the progression: cross-source metadata reconciliation (the document exists in another system under a different categorization), document re-creation from templates (for documents like statements that could be regenerated from transactional data), the RAG pipeline (for documents that existed but couldn't be associated through structured methods), and manual investigation. Gap rates varied dramatically by vintage year — loans from the most recent decade had near-complete inventories, while early-2000s vintages originated under prior institutional arrangements had significantly higher gap rates. Each resolution layer reduced the remaining gap population, and the waterfall reporting made it visible exactly how much each layer contributed.

### What We Learned

**The metadata repository is the single highest-leverage investment.** Everything downstream — gap analysis, matching, transfer preparation, completeness reporting — depends on having a centralized, normalized view of what documents exist and where they live. Building this repository was the most time-consuming phase of the program, but it made everything else possible. If you're facing a similar migration, start here.

**Exhaust cheap methods before deploying expensive ones.** The three-tier cascade wasn't just a matching strategy — it was a cost and complexity management strategy. Deterministic matching resolved 85% of the population at essentially zero marginal cost. Probabilistic scoring resolved another 12% with moderate computational effort. By the time we reached the RAG pipeline, we were operating on roughly 3% of the original volume — a 30× reduction in GenAI scope. This principle generalizes: always structure your pipeline so that the most expensive techniques are applied to the smallest, most intractable population.

**Semantic search earns its place when the problem shifts from metadata to content.** For the portion of the residual where structured metadata fields were simply too sparse to work with, reading the actual document text was the only path forward. Embedding-based semantic search let us encode noisy OCR output, partial names, and date fragments into vectors and search across the account space in ways that no rule-based system could. This is the genuine use case — not a replacement for record linkage, but a complement for the cases that record linkage can't reach.

**RAG earns its place through explainability, not just accuracy.** Running every Tier 3 document through the full RAG pipeline — retrieve, augment, generate — was a deliberate choice. We could have used cosine similarity alone for clear-winner cases and reserved the SLM for ambiguous ones. But the SLM-generated explanation is what makes the match *auditable*. An examiner reviewing the audit trail two years later can't evaluate "cosine similarity: 0.93." They can evaluate "Matched because borrower name matches OCR text, origination date is within 2 days, and SSN suffix aligns." The marginal cost of running a 7B model on all Tier 3 documents was negligible compared to the governance value of having a human-readable rationale for every AI-assisted match decision.

**Text assembly is where semantic search succeeds or fails.** The embedding model doesn't understand database fields — it understands text. Constructing labeled, consistently structured text representations for both accounts and documents was the single most impactful engineering decision in Tier 3. The labels ("Borrower:", "Origination Date:", "Doc Type:") act as semantic anchors that enable the model to recognize cross-format matches between structured account profiles and unstructured OCR output. Get the templates wrong, and similarity search returns noise. Get them right, and a scanned promissory note from 2003 lands next to the account it belongs to.

**Human-in-the-loop is not a fallback — it's a feature.** The adjudication dashboard wasn't a concession to imperfect automation. It was a deliberate design choice that produced better outcomes than either full automation or full manual review. Analysts reviewing AI-proposed matches with SLM-generated reasoning were faster and more accurate than analysts reviewing raw document metadata without context. In a regulated environment, the combination of AI-generated explanations and human approval created a governance model that neither pure automation nor pure manual review could match.

**Build the audit trail from day one.** We didn't know whether regulators would ask to see our document matching methodology six months or six years after the transfer. We built the immutable audit trail anyway — logging every decision with full lineage — because the cost of adding it later would have been orders of magnitude higher than building it in from the start. This turned out to be one of the best decisions of the program: proactive governance is always cheaper than reactive reconstruction.

**Vintage year is the strongest predictor of difficulty.** If your portfolio spans decades, expect that the oldest vintages will consume a disproportionate share of your gap resolution effort. Early-vintage loans were originated under different processes, stored in different systems, and governed by different retention policies. Build your project plan around vintage-specific complexity, not average-case assumptions.

**Match your deployment to your timeline.** This was a one-off migration, and we deployed accordingly — batch scripts, existing infrastructure, purpose-built review tooling. Investing in production-grade ML infrastructure (container orchestration, model monitoring, continuous retraining) would have consumed time we didn't have for capability we didn't need. Know the difference between building for durability and building for a deadline. Both are valid — but the engineering decisions are different.

### Conclusion

Migrating a legacy lending portfolio's document inventory — with its fragmented source systems, decades of accumulated metadata inconsistency, and near-zero tolerance for gaps — was the most complex data engineering challenge our team had faced. The structured account data migration, the part everyone worries about, was the straightforward half. The document migration, with its storage heterogeneity and completeness requirements, was where the real engineering happened.

The three-tier matching strategy — deterministic joins, probabilistic scoring, and the RAG pipeline — provided the resolution architecture and the cost management framework. The central metadata repository provided the foundation. The human-in-the-loop design ensured that AI-assisted decisions had governance, explainability, and a permanent audit trail. And the deployment approach — deliberately lightweight, matched to the one-off nature of the task — demonstrated that GenAI can deliver value in regulated environments without requiring a full production ML platform, as long as the scope is right and the humans stay in the loop.

If your organization is planning a portfolio transfer, a business line wind-down, or any large-scale document migration, the lesson from our experience is this: start with the documents, not the data. The data will follow well-worn patterns. The documents will surprise you — and the earlier you invest in understanding what you have, where it lives, and how to match it, the better your outcome will be.
